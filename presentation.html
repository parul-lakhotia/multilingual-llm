The Central Question

Does explicit morphological feature extraction
add value over what the encoder already knows?

...or is it just adding parameters for the sake of it?

How This Started

Qi et al. (2025) — MRL-POS: dual-gating co-attention + affix BiLSTM.
84.1% F1 on low-resource Turkic. Suggested LoRA as future work.
Reproduced on Hindi HDTB — MRL-POS peaked at 93.2% macro-F1.
Below all three encoder-only baselines.
Hypothesis died — Explicit affixes added parameters,
not signal, on fusional morphology.
SynPOS was born — CNN over encoder hidden states. No sub-word decomposition.
94.3% on Hindi. Clean convergence. Fewer parameters.

Two Branches, One Framework

SynPOS Branch

Parallel 1-D CNNs (kernel 3 & 5) over
XLM-R hidden states → 768-d vector

Captures local syntactic context.
No morphological decomposition. Zero cold-start.

MRL-POS Branch

Character n-gram affix extraction →
BiLSTM → intra-affix attention → 768-d

Explicit morphological features.
Akshara-level for Indic scripts. ~2 epoch cold-start.

Both → Dual-gating co-attention → Linear-chain CRF decoder
Both evaluated under Full fine-tuning and LoRA (rank-8, 1.2M params)

The Arabic Anomaly

+5.8 pp

SynPOS/LoRA over MRL-POS/Full

90.14% → 95.71%

Root-and-pattern morphology produces many short, high-frequency surface forms.

Character n-gram affixes can't meaningfully segment non-concatenative morphology.

The CNN's distributional approach over encoder states captures what affix heuristics miss.

Largest single LoRA gain in the study. LoRA amplifies SynPOS specifically on Arabic.

Typology Kinda Sorta Predicts

Consistent with hypothesis

Agglut Turkish, Finnish → MRL-POS

Non-concat Arabic → SynPOS

Breaks the prediction

Fusional Hindi → SynPOS

Fusional Urdu, Persian → MRL-POS

Three fusional languages. Two go MRL-POS, one goes SynPOS.
The label "fusional" is not fine-grained enough.

Alternative hypothesis: bound-morphology density in the treebank vocabulary may be a better predictor than typological family. Agglutinative = denser affix inventories = MRL-POS wins. Low suffix density or non-concatenative = SynPOS wins.

The Cold-Start Tax

MRL-POS/Full on Hindi, epoch 1:

80.7%

The affix BiLSTM needs ~2 epochs to learn useful representations when trained jointly with the encoder.

Consistent with Peters et al. (2018) on slow convergence
of character-level components.

SynPOS/LoRA on Hindi, epoch 1:

91.2%

CNN over hidden states starts useful immediately — no random affix embeddings to warm up.

Final: 94.26% — smooth convergence,
surpassing all encoder-only baselines.

Key Insight

XLM-RoBERTa's sub-word representations
already encode sufficient morphological information
for some fusional languages.

Explicit morphology becomes most helpful for agglutinative languages
with dense, productive affix inventories.

For non-concatenative morphology, n-gram heuristics
can't capture root-and-pattern structure — the CNN wins.

Contributions

1. Controlled comparison of MRL-POS and a new SynPOS branch across 6 typologically diverse MRLs (24 configurations).
2. LoRA integration into both branches — pursuing the parameter-efficient direction proposed by Qi et al. (2025).
3. Documentation of catastrophic collapse in Finnish MRL-POS/Full and its stabilisation under LoRA.
4. Evidence that typological category alone is insufficient to predict branch advantage — bound-morphology density proposed as alternative predictor.

Open Questions

  • Can bound-morphology density be quantified from treebank vocabularies to predict branch advantage a priori?
  • Would morphological dictionaries (instead of n-gram heuristics) rescue MRL-POS on non-concatenative Arabic?
  • Per-morpheme-type attention visualisation — which affixes does the co-attention module actually attend to?
  • Why do Urdu and Persian — fusional like Hindi — favour MRL-POS? (Script complexity? Treebank size? Affix regularity?)
